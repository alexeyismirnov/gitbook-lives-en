# MARTYR PAMPHILUS

Saint Pamphilus was a priest in Caesarea, Palestine. He devoted his life to translating, preserving, and editing biblical texts. At the end of 307 or the beginning of 308, he was arrested by pagans, tortured, and imprisoned. On February 16, 310, he was executed. The memory of Pamphilus is celebrated together with eleven other martyrs, thus creating a symbolic resemblance to the twelve apostles, or Christ with his eleven disciples before the Passion. When the original church of Hagia Sophia was enlarged by Emperor Constantius II around 340, the relics of Pamphilus and his disciples were transferred to Constantinople and placed in the basilica.

St. Pamphilus considered himself a disciple of Origen and continued his work; while Eusebius of Caesarea not only considered himself a disciple of Pamphilus, but also took his name in memory of him, becoming “Eusebius Pamphilus.” Everything would have been fine if Eusebius himself had not been on the side of the Arian heretics and, moreover, idolized Emperor Constantine, considering him a messianic figure. St. Augustine could not accept such an attitude of the “Eastern bishops and theologians” towards this emperor. He preferred Theodosius the Great to Constantine. Origen, on the other hand, was considered a heretic by many in the East. Thanks to such interrelationships, it is easier to remember the martyr Pamphilus in order to celebrate him with dignity. In churches that follow the Julian calendar, the memory of Saint Pamphilus falls on March 1, the beginning of the spring season. This is also an unexpected providence of God, helping to keep in mind the dates of the celebrations of the saints.
